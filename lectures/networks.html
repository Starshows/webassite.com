1962
"In 1962, a nuclear confrontation seemed imminent. The United States (US) and the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (USSR) were embroiled in the Cuban missile crisis.... Each country pondered post-nuclear attack scenarios. US authorities considered ways to communicate in the aftermath of a nuclear attack. How could any sort of 'command and control network' survive?"

Source: RAND Corporation Website

1962

Paul Baran, a researcher at RAND, offered a solution in the form of a distributed network using "redundancy" and "digital" technology.

1962

Leonard Kleinrock's 1962 Ph.D. dissertation at MIT, "Message Delay in Communication Nets with Storage", introduces the concept of a packet-switched network where datagrams can be sent into a distributed network in random order and reassembled at the receiver.

1963

J.C.R. Licklider addresses memorandum to the "Members and Affiliates of the Intergalactic Computer Network" that circumscribes the scenarios and issues facing a computer network.

1967

Plan for ARPANET using Interface Message Processors (IMP)
Lawrence Roberts of the MIT Lincoln Lab is commissioned to develop a plan for a digital network of computers for the US military.

1967

Honeywell H200 Computer
The same computer that would be linked to form the first computer network was used as a set for "Billion Dollar Brain", starring Michael Caine

1969

ARPANET, December 1969
Kleinrock, Baran and others connect computers at UCLA, Stanford, UC Santa Barbara and Utah to form the first network of computers communicating over a distributed network using the packet-switching protocol, Network Control Protocol (NCP).

1970

ARPANET, June 1970
Within months, the network spreads to the east coast, connecting machines at MIT, Harvard and Bolt, Beranek and Newman (BBN), a commercial company (now Raytheon BBN).

1972

ARPANET, March 1972
By 1972, the network had grown, but the original Interface Message Processors (IMPs) built by BBN that connected the host computers was proprietary to the military, and so growth was limited.

1977

ARPANET, July 1977

1971

First Killer App: Email
Ray Tomlinson sets up email system on ARPANET, inventing use of the @ symbol to identify remote user machine to send first email (“something like QWERTYUIOP") which was never stored

1978

International Packet Switched Service (IPSS)
The first international computer network using packet switching developed by the British General Post Office, Western Union International and the United States' Tymnet using the X.25 publicly available protocol

1980

Duke grad students introduce USENET, the first widespread Bulletin Board Service (BBS) that connects one-to-many
